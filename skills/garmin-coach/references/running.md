# Running

Coaching heuristics for running-specific metrics. Loaded when the user's profile includes `running`.

## Easy Run Pacing

The most common amateur mistake is running easy days too hard. Gray-zone creep erodes recovery and blunts aerobic adaptation.

- **Target HR for easy runs:** 60–70% of max HR (MAF zone).
- **Detection:** pull weekly HR zone distribution. If zone 3 time on runs labeled "easy" exceeds 20%, flag gray-zone creep.
- **Pace vs HR:** on easy days, HR is the governor, not pace. If pace has to drop to stay in zone 1–2, that's fine — it means the body needs more recovery, not that fitness is declining.
- **Conversational pace test:** if the runner can't speak in full sentences, they're too fast. This is the simplest field check.
- **Seasonal drift:** heat, altitude, fatigue, and poor sleep all raise HR at the same pace. Don't let the runner chase a pace number when conditions shift — coach HR compliance, not pace compliance.

## Volume Progression

The "10% rule" is a simplification. Use these guardrails:

- **Weekly volume:** no single week should exceed 1.5× the average of the previous 4 weeks. A 20–30% jump is the realistic safe ceiling for most amateurs.
- **Single long run:** no single run should be >10% longer (distance) than the longest run in the past 30 days.
- **Build/deload pattern:** 3 weeks of progressive build followed by 1 deload week (reduce volume by 30–40%). This is the most injury-protective pattern for amateur runners.
- **Detection:** compare this week's total distance to the 4-week rolling average. Flag if >1.3× with a caution; flag if >1.5× as injury risk.
- **Return from break:** after ≥2 weeks off, restart at 50–60% of previous volume and rebuild over 3–4 weeks. Do not jump back to prior levels.
- **Acute injury risk signals:** sudden volume spike + poor sleep scores + declining HRV = high-risk combination. Call it out clearly.

## Biomechanics

- **Cadence:** 170–185 spm optimal. Below 160 = likely overstriding.
- **Ground contact time (GCT):** lower is better. <240ms good. >280ms flag it.
- **Vertical oscillation:** lower = more efficient. <8cm good. >10cm = "bouncing."
- **Stride length:** individual metric. Increasing at same cadence = genuine improvement. Increasing with lower cadence = possibly overstriding.
- **GCT balance:** should be close to 50/50. Imbalance >2% may indicate compensation or underlying asymmetry.
- **Pace-normalized interpretation:** all biomechanics metrics change with speed. Compare at similar paces across weeks, not across workout types. A cadence of 165 at easy pace may be fine if it's 180+ at tempo. Report trends at comparable effort levels.

## Cardiac Drift & Long Run Analysis

### Cardiac drift

- Calculate from activity splits: (avg HR last third) / (avg HR first third) − 1.
- <5% in steady-state run: excellent aerobic fitness.
- 5–10%: normal.
- >10%: dehydration, insufficient base, heat stress, or accumulated fatigue.

### Temperature context

- Heat adds ~3–5 bpm per 5°C above 20°C. A 12% drift on a 32°C day may be equivalent to 6–7% in neutral conditions. Always check weather data before flagging drift as a fitness issue.
- If temperature data is available in the activity, note it alongside drift.

### Pacing strategy

- **Even splits:** first-half and second-half pace within 2–3%. Ideal for aerobic development.
- **Negative split:** second half faster than first. Strong sign of fitness and discipline. Highlight it.
- **Positive split (fade):** second half slower than first. >5% fade on a long run = likely went out too fast, underfueled, or hit aerobic ceiling.
- **Power fade in the final third:** if running power drops >10% in the last third while HR remains elevated, suspect glycogen depletion. Ask about fueling strategy for runs >90 min.

### Marathon-specific endurance

- A long run at marathon pace with <8% cardiac drift and <5% pace fade in the final quarter signals strong marathon-specific fitness.
- If the longest run shows >12% drift and >10% pace fade, marathon readiness is limited regardless of weekly volume.

## Intensity Distribution

- Zone 1–2: should be ~80% of weekly running time.
- Zone 3: the "gray zone" — minimize this. Too hard to recover from, too easy to drive adaptation.
- Zone 4–5: should be ~20% of weekly running time, concentrated in key sessions.
- If zone 3 > 30% of total running time, flag it. The most likely cause is easy runs done too fast.
- **Polarized vs threshold model:** most amateurs benefit from strict polarization (80/20). Advanced runners may use a pyramidal model (more zone 3 around race-specific blocks), but only if zone 1–2 base is already solid.

## Interval & Speed Work Analysis

### Tempo runs (lactate threshold)

- **Target:** 82–88% of max HR, sustained for 20–40 minutes.
- **Pace reference:** roughly marathon pace to half-marathon pace for most amateurs.
- **Quality check:** HR should stabilize within the first 5–8 minutes and stay within a 5-bpm band. If HR keeps climbing throughout, the pace is too aggressive or fatigue is too high.

### VO2max intervals

- **Target:** >90% of max HR for work intervals of 3–5 minutes. Common formats: 5×1000m, 4×1200m, 3×1600m.
- **Time at >90% HRmax:** total time in the session above 90% HRmax is the key stimulus metric. 10–15 min total at >90% is a strong session.
- **Rep consistency:** compare pace and HR across reps. Pace fading >5% from first to last rep while HR is already maxed = the workout exceeded current capacity. Note it but don't alarm — this is how limits are found.
- **Recovery between reps:** HR should drop to 70–75% HRmax during recovery jogs. If HR stays above 80% into the next rep, recovery intervals are too short or cumulative fatigue is too high.

### Speed/repetition work

- **Short reps (200m–400m):** neuromuscular stimulus. HR is less meaningful here — look at pace consistency and cadence. These should feel controlled, not maximal.
- **Strides:** 80–100m at ~95% effort with full recovery. Cadence should be >185 spm. Flag if cadence is notably lower than in longer intervals.

### General interval quality signals

- Consistent splits = well-calibrated effort.
- Progressive slowdown with maxed HR = session was at or beyond current limit.
- Unusually slow reps with low HR = the runner was already fatigued going in (check prior days' load and sleep).

## Taper & Freshness

Tapering confuses Garmin metrics. Coach the runner on what to expect:

- **Volume reduction:** 40–60% volume reduction over 10–14 days for a marathon, 7–10 days for a half-marathon, 5–7 days for shorter races.
- **Intensity preservation:** maintain 1–2 short, sharp sessions per week during taper. Drop volume, not intensity.
- **HRV during taper:** HRV may initially dip in the first 3–5 days of taper (the body is recalibrating, not declining). It should stabilize or rise in the final 4–7 days. A sustained drop through the entire taper = something else is wrong (illness, stress, poor sleep).
- **RHR during taper:** RHR may rise 2–5 bpm during taper. This is normal — reduced training volume shifts autonomic balance. Do not flag a small RHR rise during a planned taper as a concern.
- **Garmin training status:** "Detraining" or "Recovery" labels are expected during taper. Do not alarm the runner. Reframe it: "Garmin sees reduced load, but this is intentional freshening."
- **Race readiness signals:** HRV stable or rising + RHR stable or slightly elevated + sleep scores >80 + body battery morning charge >70 = ready.

## Environmental Factors

### Heat

- HR increases ~3–5 bpm per 5°C above 20°C at the same effort. Paces will slow.
- **Optimal racing temperature:** 7–15°C. Above 20°C, expect measurable performance decline.
- **Coaching action:** on hot days, discount pace-based performance assessments. A "slow" easy run at 33°C may represent excellent HR discipline. Praise it.
- If a run shows high drift + slow pace but excellent HR compliance, note: "performance adjusted for heat — execution was good."

### Altitude

- Above 1500m, expect 5–10% performance decline for the first 10–14 days.
- Full acclimatization takes 3–4 weeks. Partial benefits appear after 7–10 days.
- **Coaching action:** if the runner's location or activity data indicates altitude, adjust expectations and note it in analysis.

### Humidity

- High humidity (>70%) impairs evaporative cooling, compounding heat effects. A 25°C day at 90% humidity can feel like 30°C+ in physiological load. Note when humidity data is available.

## Training Monotony

- **Concept:** doing the same volume with more variety (easy/hard alternation, different workout types) produces lower injury risk than uniform daily loads.
- **Simplified detection:** if 5+ days in a week have nearly identical distance (within 10%) and effort (within 5% HR), flag monotony. Recommend varying the stimulus: an interval day, a long run, easy days of different lengths.
- **TRIMP-based monotony index** (daily TRIMP std dev / daily TRIMP mean): values >2.0 indicate high strain with low variety. This is a conceptual flag — exact TRIMP calculation from Garmin data requires estimating zone-weighted load, so treat it as directional.

## Running Power

Garmin wrist-based running power is available on newer watches. It adds a complementary signal to HR, especially where HR is unreliable (short efforts, hills, cold weather).

### Power zones

- Zones are anchored to **Critical Power (CP)**, roughly analogous to lactate threshold power.
- If the runner has a recent threshold test or hard 20–30 min effort, estimate CP from that session's average power.
- Zone mapping (% of CP):
  - Recovery: <75%
  - Easy/aerobic: 75–88%
  - Tempo: 88–95%
  - Threshold: 95–105%
  - VO2max: 105–120%
  - Anaerobic: >120%

### Power-to-HR decoupling

- Same concept as cardiac drift, but using power: (HR/power ratio in second half) / (HR/power ratio in first half) − 1.
- <5%: excellent aerobic efficiency. Power and HR are tracking together.
- >10%: aerobic ceiling reached. This is often a cleaner signal than pace-based drift because power accounts for terrain.

### Hill pacing

- Power is more useful than HR on hills because HR lags effort by 30–60 seconds. On a short climb, HR may not peak until the runner is already cresting.
- **Coaching action:** for hilly runs, analyze power consistency over climbs rather than pace or HR. Steady power across elevation changes = good pacing. Power spikes on hills followed by deep drops on descents = surging — costs more energy overall.

### Power consistency in intervals

- Within a set of intervals, power should be consistent (within 3–5% across reps). Power is a better real-time metric than pace for interval quality because it responds instantly to effort changes.
- Declining power with rising HR across reps = genuine fatigue accumulation. Same power with stable HR = well-calibrated session.

## Weekly Report Workout Analysis

For the weekly report's key workout section, analyze each significant run:

- **Splits:** even pacing, negative split, or fade? Quantify the delta.
- **Cardiac drift:** calculate from first-third vs last-third avg HR. Contextualize with temperature.
- **Power-to-HR decoupling:** if power data available, report alongside cardiac drift.
- **Top sessions by training effect:** distance, duration, avg pace, avg/max HR, avg power (if available), cadence, GCT, vertical oscillation.
- **Interval sessions:** rep-by-rep consistency, time at >90% HRmax, recovery HR between reps.
- **Long runs:** pacing strategy, drift, power fade, and fueling signals.
- **Biomechanics:** report week averages for cadence, GCT, vertical oscillation, stride length. Compare vs 4-week average at comparable paces. Spotlight the ONE metric moving most notably.
- **Volume safety check:** compare this week's total distance to the 4-week rolling average. Note if the long run exceeded 10% over the longest run in the past 30 days.
