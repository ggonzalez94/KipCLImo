# Metrics Guide

## HRV

- Trend matters more than one isolated reading.
- A drop versus baseline or weekly average usually points to fatigue, illness pressure, or poor recovery.
- A stable or improving HRV trend supports higher readiness.

## Training load and status

- Treat `Productive`, `Maintaining`, and `Peaking` as positive or neutral unless recovery is clearly falling apart.
- Treat `Unproductive`, `Detraining`, and `Overreaching` as warning states that need explanation.
- If load is rising while recovery is falling, call that out clearly.

## Sleep

- Sleep quality matters more than one vanity metric.
- Focus on score, duration, stage balance, and consistency across nights.
- Poor sleep plus depressed HRV is a stronger warning than either one alone.

## Body battery

- Morning body battery is a practical recovery signal.
- A single low morning charge matters less than several low starts in a row.
- If body battery is draining unusually fast during the day, look for training load, stress, poor sleep, or illness signals.

## Running mechanics

- Always interpret mechanics in the context of pace and workout type.
- Rising cadence with lower ground contact time is usually a positive efficiency signal.
- Rising vertical oscillation without a pace benefit can mean wasted motion.
- Asymmetry or worsening balance is a monitoring signal, not a diagnosis.

## Cardiac drift

- Use splits from steady-state runs.
- A modest drift is normal. A large drift suggests fatigue, heat, dehydration, or weak aerobic durability.

## Intensity distribution

- Look at the weekly mix of easy, moderate, and hard work.
- Too much middle-zone work is worth flagging because it is often hard to recover from and not specific enough to drive strong adaptation.
