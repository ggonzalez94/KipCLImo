# Gym

Coaching heuristics for strength and gym training. Loaded when the user's profile includes `gym`.

IMPORTANT: If gym data is not properly tracked(e.g. sessions are recorded, but not specific reps and workouts) don't pay as much attention to this, and just focus on the number and duration of the sessions.

## Volume Tracking

- Track sets × reps × load per muscle group per week. Total weekly volume is the primary driver of adaptation.
- **Minimum effective volume:** ~10 sets/week per muscle group for maintenance. ~15–20 sets/week for hypertrophy/strength gains.
- **Diminishing returns:** above ~25 sets/week per muscle group. Flag if the athlete is consistently exceeding this without a planned overreach rationale.
- When Garmin activity data is available for strength sessions, use duration and session count as proxies for volume trends. If detailed set/rep/load data is absent, ask the user or infer from logged notes.

## Progressive Overload

- The key signal: is load, volume, or density increasing over 4-week windows?
- Stagnation for >3 weeks on a compound lift = flag it. Ask whether technique issues, fatigue accumulation, or lack of periodization is the likely cause.
- **Acceptable progression rate for intermediates:** 1–2% load increase per week on major lifts.
- Density (same volume in less time) counts as progress. Don't fixate only on load.
- Regression during a peak sport block is expected and not a flag — context matters.

## Complementary Strength for Runners and Cyclists

- **Runners:** prioritize single-leg work (lunges, step-ups, single-leg deadlifts), hip stability (lateral band work, Copenhagen planks), and core anti-rotation (Pallof press, dead bugs). These directly transfer to running economy and injury resilience.
- **Cyclists:** emphasize posterior chain (glutes, hamstrings). Cyclists are already quad-dominant; gym work should redress the balance. Include hip hinge patterns and single-leg pressing.
- **Session frequency:**
  - Base phase: 2–3 sessions/week
  - Peak/race phase: 1–2 sessions/week, reduced volume and intensity
- Flag if gym volume is high during a hard training block in the primary sport. The combined load may compromise sport-specific adaptation and recovery.

## Recovery Impact

- Heavy strength sessions elevate training readiness cost for 24–48 hours. Treat a hard gym session as a significant training stress, not a "rest day."
- **Watch for:**
  - HRV dropping on the morning after gym sessions
  - Body battery staying low (<40) the day after a gym session
  - Declining training readiness scores on gym-following days
  - Poor sleep scores after evening gym sessions (sympathetic activation)
- If these patterns cluster around gym days, the combined weekly load is likely too high. Recommend either reducing gym volume, shifting sessions to morning, or adding a recovery day between gym and key sport sessions.
- Encourage the athlete to log strength sessions on Garmin so training load and recovery metrics can be correlated properly.
